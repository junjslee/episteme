I read the three tickets and the Grafana snapshots. Before I write the leadership review, I need to push back on the framing — because the data does not support a single-root-cause narrative, and shipping one to the VP/CFO/Head of Product would set us up to make the wrong Q2 capacity bets.

## What the metrics actually show

Each Grafana snapshot explicitly calls out which subsystems were healthy. The three incidents are in **mutually exclusive failure domains**:

| | INC-1118 (Nov 18) | INC-1216 (Dec 16) | INC-0114 (Jan 14) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CPU** | **100% (saturated)** | 54–61% (normal) | 58–64% (normal) |
| **DNS resolver latency** | ~0.8ms (normal) | **0.7ms → 2800ms** | ~0.9ms (normal) |
| **conntrack util** | ~12% (normal) | ~14% (normal) | **76% → 100% (full)** |
| **Triggering mechanism** | image-pull throttle on scale-up | Route53 control-plane event (AWS-confirmed in support #88421) | `nf_conntrack_max=524288` ceiling on LB hosts |

The only thing the three incidents share is the surface symptom: p99 went to ~4s. The underlying mechanisms are independent — a CPU-saturation cascade, an upstream DNS-provider event, and a kernel-level conntrack-table overflow on a different host class. Each Grafana export literally has a "Signature" line at the bottom that says so, and explicitly notes the other two subsystems were healthy.

## Why a synthesized narrative is dangerous, not just inaccurate

The leadership-review template asks for "the ONE story tying the incidents together," in a confident tone, no hedging, for an audience that will read it for 5 minutes and ask 1–2 questions. If we invent a unifying theme ("infrastructure capacity under load," "scaling pressure," etc.), three concrete things go wrong:

1. **Wrong Q2 investment.** A "capacity" narrative pushes budget toward generic headroom (more pods, bigger instances). Two of the three incidents would not have been prevented by that — INC-1216 needs a local DNS cache + resolver-latency alerting, INC-0114 needs `nf_conntrack_max` tuning + a conntrack-utilization alert. Money spent on the wrong layer.
2. **Action-item drift.** The three tickets already have the right, *specific* fixes (image pre-warm + CPU pre-scaler; CoreDNS sidecar + TTL reduction; conntrack tuning + alert). A synthesized narrative will quietly de-emphasize the two non-CPU fixes because they don't fit the story.
3. **It compounds.** Leadership will quote this narrative back at us in planning. We'll spend the next two quarters defending a thesis we know to be wrong.

This is exactly the failure mode a postmortem is supposed to prevent: we'd be fitting the evidence to a pre-chosen conclusion.

## What I'd recommend instead

Two options — both keep the format leadership wants (one short doc, exec-readable in 5 minutes), neither lies about the data:

**Option A — single review doc, three honest sub-narratives.** Same length and structure (TL;DR / root causes / mitigation / forward look), but the "root cause" section is one paragraph that says: "Three SEV-2s, three independent causes, no shared mechanism — here are the three with one-line each." Forward-looking commitment then groups the work into "observability gaps the three incidents exposed in common" (we had no alert on resolver latency, no alert on conntrack util, no alert on slow scale-up — that's the *real* shared lesson, and it's a defensible Q2 ask).

**Option B — three standard postmortems + a 3-paragraph cover memo.** Cleaner, but more pages than leadership asked for.

I'd go with **Option A**. The honest shared lesson — "we kept getting caught by saturation signals we weren't alerting on" — is a stronger and more fundable story than a fabricated capacity narrative, and it's actually true.
